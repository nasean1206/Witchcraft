Supplemental Readings

Salem Possessed: The Social Origins of Witchcraft (1976)

Within this work, historians Paul Boyer and Stephen Nissenbaum contextualize the harsh factional divides and social intricacies that manifestested in the Salem Witch Trials. The point is made that this witch outbreak in Massachusetts was not an isolated event, but rather that it represented the transformation of American culture: the crumbling of Puritanism, the pressure of limited land and resources, the weakening of religion, and the rising tide of commercial capitalism.

The Possession at Loudun (2000)

August 18, 1634 saw the prosecution and confession of Father Urbain Grandier for engaging in the bewitchment of Ursuline nuns in Loudun. This tragic case climaxed in the gruesome torture and execution of Grandier at the hands of his own exorcists. In an intersection of primary documents and thought-provoking social commentary, Michel de Certeau's The Possession at Loudun recounts the manifestation of diabolism and witchcraft that culminated in the crisis at Loudun.

  • More

Deadly Words: Witchcraft in the Bocage (1981)

The witchcraft phenomenon within the bocage, a rural area of western France, is examined by Jeanne Favret-Saada. Primary sources of the bewitched are utilized to illustrate the tense sociopolitical atmosphere; paranoia is in the air as the witch is somebody within the society, and only an "unwitcher" has the ability to break the misfortunes cast.

  • More

The Night Battles: Witchcraft & Agrarian Cults in the Sixteenth & Seventeenth Centuries (1966)

The Benandanti were a peasant fertility c ult in Friuli that microhistorian Carlo Ginzburg studies in great detail. The Night Battles offers Ginzburg's fresh take that explains a narrow piece of the witchcraft craze, a phenomenon in the early modern era that was pervasive throughotu Europe. Ginzburg argues that inquisitors mistakenly attribute these Benandanti and their pagan rituals to the heretic crimes of witchcraft and devil worship.

  • More

The Bewitching of Anne Gunter: A Horrible and True Story of Deception, Witchcraft, Murder, and the King of England (2001)

A story of manipulative fathers, obeying daughters, power divides between classes, and village life in early-modern Europe, James Sharpe takes a look at the witchcraft craze during the 17th century through the fabricated possession of Anne Gunter. Gruesome symptoms including foaming at the mouth, contorting wildly in bed, and vomiting pins proved to be terrifyingly convincing in feeding the witchcraft craze during this era.

  • More

Monsters of the Gévaudan: The Making of a Beast (2011)

1764 saw the mutilation of a peasant girl who was tending a flock of sheep. More than a hundred soon became victims of the mythical creature that terrorized 18th century Europe. Jay M. Smith attempts to get to the bottom of these unanswered questions: What exactly was this beast of the Gévaudan? Why did it prey mainly on women and children? Why did society think the beast was more than just a wolf?

Robert St. George is Associate Professor of History. His research explores ethnographic method, folklife studies, material culture, vernacular landscapes, and heritage productions in North America, England, Ireland, and Iceland. He teaches undergraduate courses on such topics as early American cultural history, witchcraft in the early modern world, public culture, American vernacular architecture, performing history, and American consumer culture. He is a graduate of Hamilton College (A.B., 1976), the Winterthur Program in Early American Culture at the University of Delaware (M.A., 1978), and the University of Pennsylvania (M.A., 1980, Ph.D., 1982).
