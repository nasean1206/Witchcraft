Criteria of the Witch

Based on John Demos' "Entertaining Satan: Witchcraft and the Culture of Early New England"

  1. Was female
  2. Was of middle age (between forty and sixty years old
  3. Was of English (and "Puritan") background
  4. Was married, but was more likely (than the general population to have few children -- or none at all
  5. Was frequently involved in trouble and conflict with other family members
  6. Had been accused, on some previous occasion, of committing crimes -- most especially theft, slander, or other forms of assaultive speech
  7. Was more likely (than the general population) to have professed and practiced a medical vocation, i.e. "doctoring" on a local, quite informal basis
  8. Was of relatively low social position
  9. Was abrasive in style, contentious in character, and stubbornly resilient in the face of adversity

Witches' Mark

According to witch-hunters such as Matthew Hopkins, the witches' mark is a permanent marking of the Devil that indicates an individual's allegiance to diabolism and witchcraft. The mark is made by the Devil -- either through mutilation of flesh by his claw or through branding using a hot iron. Raging in the 16th and 17th century, the witch craze was fueled by unsubstantiated claims and arbitrary physical blemishes such as that of the witches' mark.

The Familiar

Serving as a witch's obedient servant, a famiiar is an animal spirit that helps in bewitching enemies and performing rituals. Generally possessing magicical abilities, it is believed that familiars have the capability of shapeshifting. The familiar is an distinctive characteristic of witchcraft in the Early modern period in England during British witch trials; however, its appearance is a rare sight in New World (American) witchcrat.

Familiars, derived from the Latin word Familiaris (defined as "household servant"), are referred to in the Holy Bible, as spirit guides for sorcerers and necromancers.

“A man also or woman that hath a familiar spirit, or that is a wizard, shall surely be put to death: they shall stone them with stones: their blood shall be upon them” (Leviticus, 20:27).

The animal manifestations of familiars generally included cats, mice, ferrets, hares, bats, snakes, dogs, or birds (specifically ravens & owls) -- the most common were black cats or black dogs. Names chosen were of the strange and wicked sorts such as Pyewacket or Dandiprat. Because accused witches and cunning folk in the Middle Ages tended to be ostracized and separated from society, they often kept small animal companions around their home; unfortunately, owning a black cat was grounds for witchcraft investigations.

Reciting the Lord's Prayer

The accused witch is put to the test, either during trial or by the gallows, to recite the Lord's Prayer without error. Stumbling, stuttering, or stammering were grounds for confirmation that the accused was, in fact, a witch. Although most humans would erroneously recite the Lord's Prayer, the prevailing belief was that any mistake made under "God's eyes" (and the accuser's eyes) are unacceptable. Unfortunately, accused persons who have medical conditions (retardation, hysterical, or even hallucinations from LSD-fungus-covered bread) are put at an unfair disadvantage in having to read the Bible without error.
