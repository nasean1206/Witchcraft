The North Berwick Trials

One of the most infamous cases that intertwined witch hunting and monarch politics was that of the North Berwick Trials that began in Scotland in November 1590. Geillis Duncan was accused of practicing witchcraft by her master, David Seton. Because Duncan was locally famed for being a healer, Seton’s suspicions of witchcraft were further solidified. Seton went on to torture Duncan based on his suspicions, forcing her to admit to witchcraft along with naming her accomplices – this led to the start of the first large-scale witch hunt in Scotland.

Notable about the North Berwick trials was the involvement of King James VI of Scotland because of the accused stating that the Devil was intended to overthrow or kill him. In the past, King James VI believed that his misfortunes were due to the meddling of witches, such as the storms that prevented the arrival of Princess Anne of Denmark, the king’s new bride. Some accused witches admitted to causing these storms, among other misfortunes.

The North Berwick Trials was the first case in Scottish witchcraft history that concluded some semblance of a witch’s Sabbath, as these accused witches made confessions of meeting in large conspiring groups. Contradicting patterns in other parts of Europe however, the witches did not partake in obscene sexual activities. Rather, the witches confessed to partaking in practicing the dark arts of the Malleus Maleficarum and devising heretical acts.

After these trials were concluded, a pamphlet Newes from Scotland was published and distributed; in it contained the King’s involvement in the North Berwick Trials and his personal beliefs in the legitimacy of witchcraft. This pamphlet went on to become a valuable resource in justifying and accepting witch hunts and witchcraft trials.

Sourced from Brian P. Levack, author of Witch-Hunting in Scotland: Law, Politics, and Religion. New York: Routledge, 2008.
